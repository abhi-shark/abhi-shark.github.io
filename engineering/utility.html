Overview

This project applies Expected Utility Theory to evaluate and compare vehicle purchase options for a graduate student. The goal is to analyze how various vehicle attributes contribute to the decision-making process and identify the most optimal vehicle based on individual preferences and constraints.

Decision Context

As a first-time car owner and a mechanical engineering graduate student at Purdue University, I aimed to find a vehicle that balances cost, reliability, and performance. Key considerations included minimizing initial and ongoing costs, maximizing safety and technology features, and ensuring comfort for daily commutes and occasional road trips.

Key Objectives

    Fundamental Objectives

  • Minimize Costs: Initial purchase price, monthly maintenance, and fuel economy.
  • Ensure Safety and Technology: Features like ABS, traction control, and blind spot monitoring.
  • Optimize Performance: Comfortable driving dynamics, sufficient engine power, and reliability.
  • Maximize Usability: Adequate cargo and passenger space in a compact design.

    • Means Objectives

  • Reduce tax and insurance burdens.
  • Prioritize fuel efficiency and manageable maintenance.
  • Choose user-friendly features for new drivers.
  • Favor vehicles with minimal depreciation.

Methodology

The decision-making process involved:

  • Defining Attributes: Attributes like purchase cost, maintenance costs, fuel economy, and engine displacement were quantified.
  • Utility Functions: Created single-attribute and multi-attribute utility functions to calculate and rank alternatives.
  • Evaluation of Alternatives: Compared four vehicles using real-world data and simulations
    • 2018 Subaru Legacy
    • 2014 Toyota Camry
    • 2016 Toyota Avalon
    • 2013 Honda Accord

The attributes were evaluated using Expected Utility Theory, allowing for a structured comparison of options under uncertainty and ensuring alignment with the decision-maker's objectives.

Highlights & Results

Multi-Attribute Utility Scores:

  • 2014 Toyota Camry: 0.501
  • 2016 Toyota Avalon: 0.417
  • 2013 Honda Accord: 0.405
  • 2018 Subaru Legacy: 0.324
The 2014 Toyota Camry emerged as the top choice due to its balanced performance, cost, and reliability.

Learnings:

The analysis underscored the importance of structured decision-making. By applying Expected Utility Theory, I was able to evaluate alternatives systematically and validate my preferences. The framework can be extended to include additional variables, such as depreciation and mileage, for a more comprehensive analysis.
