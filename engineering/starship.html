Starship Transformer Toy Design Project

Timeline

August - December 2022

Technologies

PTC Creo - CAD
KiCAD - PCB Design
Blynk - IoT Platform

Role

Electronics and Software Lead

Overview

This project was done as part of a capstone toy-design class, where we learned the fundamentals of rapid prototyping and design thinking in meeting the needs of stakeholders with a set of requirements(in this case, children). The project involved designing a toy that was safe, interactive and engaging for children aged 5-10 years. The Starship Transformer theme was chosen due to Starship robots being an iconic part of campus life at Purdue, as well as the Transformers franchise being a large part of the team's childhood.

Watch our Project showcase video!

Key Features

  • The robot is to be safe and engaging to children, with no risk of harm from sharp edges, small parts or exposed batteries
  • The robot can be transformed from a compact 'Starship robot' form to a humanoid 'Transformer' form that can stand upright
  • The robot can be remotely locomoted using Blynk on the user's mobile phone, and can store small items like a real Starship robot within a compartment on its back.
  • The robot behaves and sounds like a real Starship robot, with sound effects and LED lights for headlights and taillights.

Technical Implementation

The Starship Transformer Robot was implemented using a mechanical structure featuring seven major parts connected via six pin joints to enable transformation between starship and humanoid forms. The body served as the main housing, storing the electronics in a cavity, while the head, arms, and hip pieces provided functionality and aesthetic appeal. The arms and head were designed to move within 90 degrees of freedom, while the hip facilitated additional movement for standing posture. The drivetrain, powered by motors in the legs and designed with six wheels (two powered, four free-spinning), enabled differential steering. Components were designed using CAD software, with joints and cavities integrated into the design for seamless assembly and functionality.

The electronic system included an ESP32 microcontroller, a motor controller, a 9V battery, and LEDs for headlights and taillights. The system was controlled wirelessly through the Blynk app, enabling remote operation of the robot. A secondary circuit within the head housed a speaker system for sound effects. Due to hardware limitations, the system required a USB power source, impacting the full functionality during testing. Prototyping was achieved through 3D printing, and design-for-manufacturing principles were applied to reduce costs while maintaining functionality. Challenges with electronic power management and joint interference were identified, with recommendations for stiffer joints, better power solutions, and optimized wiring for future iterations.

The final product implemented these improvements by using a drone battery with a custom power-control circuit to drive the motors at a higher voltage and current. Joint issues were resolved by using a combination of screws, pins and reprints with additional support structures to increase structural stiffness. Wiring was optimized by soldering components together and bundling wires before routing, minimizing the amount of wiring used.

Results & Impact

All key features were achieved. The product was printed in black and white filament looking nearly identical to the real Starship robots on campus. The robot was able to successfully locomote remotely, and was able to store small items like candy in its storage compartment. Components and wiring was unaffected by the transformation feature, and power delivery was reliable and safe. The project was well-received by the class and the professor, and was a hit with children who visited the showcase. The toy was also recognized as one of the top projects in the showcase.
